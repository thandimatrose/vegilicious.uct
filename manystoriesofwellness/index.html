The Many Stories of Wellness: Exploring Ecolinguistics

Reflecting on Dr Arran Stibbe's Appearance on the Sentientism Podcast

“The stories we live by are encoded in language. These stories shape our relationship with the natural world, determining whether we exploit it or care for it.”

In this episode of the Sentientism podcast, Dr. Arron Stibbe, Professor of Ecological Linguistics at the University of Gloucestershire, discusses how his work in discourse analysis and ecolinguistics offers insights into shaping more ethical, sustainable societies. This is particularly relevant to the reason we're brought together: plant-based eating. The discussion presents interesting considerations regarding how we conceptualize the stories of our plant-eating habits and how we express them to others.

For a good portion of the episode, Stibbe makes a point of outlining his expansive definition of what a story is. He describes stories as "reservoirs of meaning", an important characterization in understanding how Stibbe links language to behaviour He lays out his and other ecological linguistics scholars’ mandate to create an explicit ecological philosophy or “ecosophy,” a philosophy shaped by their scholarship on wellness and well-being. This forms the standard against which a text is then evaluated as either beneficial or detrimental.

To express some of Stibbe’s beliefs on the matter, his research rejects the dichotomy that narratives are either true or subjective. Moreover, socially constructed realities, in his view, are at play everywhere and all the time. Throughout the episode, he also emphasizes the interconnectivity that exists between humans, other living beings, and the environment. His ecosophy is simply:

Living!

One genre of story that encapsulates this ecosophy is indigenous knowledge systems. At the mention of indigeneity in this discussion, I thought about this video by Andrew Sage, the creator of the Andrewism YouTube channel, where he outlines two definitions of indigeneity: indigeneity by means of colonial relationship and indigeneity by means of land relationship. These are important definitions. From the conversation, I got the impression that Stibbe was using the latter definition.

However, in the South African context, where the land and the colonial relationship are intricately intertwined for Black and other racialized people, our stories, including those that concern our political and fundamentally human wills, are required in moving our country in an ecologically beneficial direction. A great example of this is discussed in this episode of the Knowing Animals podcast, where Dr. Esther Alloun from the University of Wollongong presents her research on the animal rights movements in Palestine and Israel, examining how their positionality in the settler colonial dynamic gave rise to very different modes of activism.

Colonialism imposed many stories of subjugation, one of which is the chapter called Capitalism, a concept referred to in this discussion as a “macro story”, a big, destructive story at that. In its pervasiveness in the shared socially constructed reality, it actualizes behaviours such as centering oneself rather than the collective, prioritizing economic growth at all costs, and commodifying and objectifying everything outside of ourselves. This story also dehumanizes us, creating a world where we operate and are treated as consumers.

Our individual decisions to pursue a more plant-based diet are defined by a care ethic, environmental concern, religion, or other beliefs, but they largely manifest as choices about consumption, and if it is the sole manifestation, it is little more than virtue signaling through conspicuous consumption. This is why the title of this episode is so salient:

Being more rather than having more.

Stibbe describes one method he uses in implementing his ecosophy as a tool to analyze stories. He examines who and what occupy what he calls "activated roles" in a text and who or what occupy passive roles. Life is movement, doing, action, and so positioning someone or something as inactive is life-denying—both in the text and the reality it reflects and influences.

What I particularly like about this measure is that it’s a fairly simple heuristic to apply to our academic essays and reports. It can reveal implicit stories nestled within our explicit arguments that we may not otherwise notice.

In reflection, this episode has encouraged me to try and notice the stories I tell in my personal life. They aren’t all intricately hidden. They are in the small things, the mundane things, the obvious things, and the arbitrary things. It’s about when I tell an event organizer that I can bring my own vegan meal and the story/ies I reconstruct in that behaviour. It’s a story of complacency. It’s also a story of visible difference. It’s also a story of my relationship, as a woman, with being “inconvenient.” It’s a story of the accessibility of vegan products. It’s a story about my commitment to my own ecosophy.

Noticing the stories that connect all the features of our reality is the first step in creating new ripples, adding new wildlife to the macro stories—these vast oceans of meaning. It’s a curiously wonderful way for thought to be actionable. And this should be our action: identifying our stories, rewriting them, creating new stories based on our own ecosophies, and most importantly, sharing them!

I hope you enjoyed my food for thought!

About the Podcast and Guest

Dr Arran Stibbe
Professor of Ecological Linguistics at the University of Gloucestershire
Stibbe’s research examines how language encodes the stories we live by, and shapes how we see ourselves and our relationship with other animals and the earth. This involves linguistic analysis of a wide range of discourses, from advertising which encourages people to buy unnecessary and ecologically damaging products, to the inspirational language of nature writing. He is the founder of the International Ecolinguistics Association.
